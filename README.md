## Overview

Poltergeist phenomena represent one of the most dramatic and controversial forms of alleged paranormal activity. The term comes from German *poltern* (to make noise, rumble) and *geist* (ghost, spirit), literally meaning "noisy ghost."

Unlike traditional hauntings attributed to spirits of the deceased, poltergeist activity is characterized by:

- **Physical disturbances**: Objects moving, levitating, or being thrown
- **Unexplained sounds**: Knocking, rapping, banging, footsteps
- **Electrical anomalies**: Lights flickering, appliances malfunctioning
- **Temperature changes**: Sudden cold or hot spots
- **Focal person pattern**: Activity typically centers around a specific individual, usually an adolescent

Poltergeist cases have been documented for over a millennium and continue to be reported globally. They present a unique challenge to both paranormal and skeptical investigators, generating theories ranging from disembodied spirits to unconscious psychokinesis to elaborate fraud.

**Key Distinction**: Approximately 90% of poltergeist cases involve an identifiable living "agent" or focal person—most commonly an adolescent experiencing psychological or emotional stress. This agent-focused pattern distinguishes poltergeists from traditional location-based hauntings.

## Etymology & Names

### Primary Name
- **Poltergeist** — German compound word: *poltern* (to make noise, rumble, crash) + *geist* (ghost, spirit). First appeared in German folklore in the 16th century; entered English in the 19th century.

### Alternative Names

| Name | Context | Meaning |
|------|---------|---------|
| RSPK | Scientific terminology | Recurrent Spontaneous Psychokinesis (parapsychological theory) |
| Noisy Ghost | English translation | Direct translation of German term |
| Knocker | English folklore | Spirit that produces knocking sounds |

## Characteristics

### Physical Manifestations

| Type | Description | Frequency | Severity |
|------|-------------|-----------|----------|
| **Object movement** | Items moving, levitating, or thrown without apparent cause | Very common | Variable |
| **Unexplained sounds** | Knocking, rapping, banging, footsteps, scratching | Very common | Low-Moderate |
| **Electrical disturbances** | Lights flickering, appliances turning on/off | Common | Low-Moderate |
| **Temperature anomalies** | Sudden cold/hot spots, temperature fluctuations | Common | Low |
| **Physical attacks** | Pushing, scratching, biting, striking witnesses | Rare | Moderate-High |
| **Spontaneous fires** | Small fires or scorch marks without ignition source | Rare | High |
| **Water phenomena** | Unexplained water, puddles, dripping sounds | Occasional | Low |
| **Stone throwing** | Rocks thrown, often appearing from nowhere | Occasional | Moderate |

### Behavioral Patterns

| Aspect | Description |
|--------|-------------|
| **Onset** | Usually gradual, beginning with minor disturbances |
| **Progression** | Typically escalates over days, weeks, or months |
| **Duration** | Usually 2-6 months; can last days to years in extreme cases |
| **Termination** | Often ends abruptly without explanation |
| **Activity Period** | Can occur any time; some cases show nocturnal preference |
| **Focus** | Centers on specific person (agent) in 90% of cases |
| **Intelligence** | Sometimes responds to requests or shows purpose; other times random |

### The Focal Person (Agent)

The phenomenon typically centers around a specific individual with common characteristics:

**Demographics**:
- **Peak age**: 12-15 years (puberty/adolescence)
- **Gender**: Female slightly more common, but both occur
- **Awareness**: Usually unaware of being the focus initially

**Psychological Profile**:
- Experiencing psychological or emotional stress
- Family conflict or tension in home environment
- Undergoing puberty or hormonal changes
- Repressed emotions or inability to express feelings
- History of trauma or abuse (in some cases)
- Activity often follows agent to new locations
- Phenomena typically cease when agent matures or stress resolves

## Notable Cases

### Enfield Poltergeist (1977-1979) ⭐

> **Location**: 284 Green Street, Enfield, North London, UK
> **Duration**: Approximately 18 months
> **Focal Person**: Janet Hodgson (age 11)
> **Investigators**: Maurice Grosse & Guy Lyon Playfair (SPR)

**The Case**: One of the most famous and extensively documented poltergeist cases in history. Activity centered on the Hodgson family, particularly daughters Janet (11) and Margaret (13). Over 1,500 incidents were documented including:

- Furniture moving without apparent cause
- Objects thrown across rooms
- Knockings and rappings
- Disembodied voice speaking through Janet (gruff male voice calling itself "Bill")
- Alleged levitations
- Physical attacks (hair pulling, pushing)
- Fires starting spontaneously
- Water puddles appearing
- Cold breezes
- Apparitions

**Key Evidence**:
- Police constable Carolyn Heeps witnessed chair sliding across floor
- Audio recordings of voice phenomena and knockings
- Photographs (some disputed)
- Multiple independent witness testimony
- 18 months of investigator documentation

**Controversy**: While extensively documented, some incidents were admitted as pranks by Janet and Margaret (particularly levitation photographs). Investigators believed majority of phenomena were genuine despite some trickery. Voice phenomena suspected as ventriloquism by skeptics.

**Credibility**: Medium-High — Multiple credible witnesses including police; professional investigation and extensive documentation; some proven hoaxes reduce credibility but don't explain all phenomena.

**Cultural Impact**: Inspired numerous books, documentaries, and films including *The Conjuring 2* (2016). Remains most famous poltergeist case.

### Rosenheim Poltergeist (1967-1968)

> **Location**: Law office, Rosenheim, Bavaria, Germany
> **Duration**: 2-3 months
> **Focal Person**: Annemarie Schneider (age 19, secretary)
> **Investigators**: Hans Bender, Friedbert Karger (physicist), electrical engineers

**The Case**: Notable for extensive scientific investigation and focus on electrical phenomena. Activity occurred in law office centered on 19-year-old secretary:

- Telephones ringing without calls
- Massive unexplained phone bills (600+ calls to speaking clock)
- Light bulbs exploding and unscrewing
- Fluorescent lights swinging
- Electrical meters spinning wildly
- Power surges without load
- Photocopier fluid displacement
- Pictures rotating on walls
- Drawers opening spontaneously

**Key Evidence**:
- Electrical monitoring data documenting anomalies
- Phone company records (600+ documented calls)
- Video recordings of swinging lights
- Power company investigation reports
- Scientific measurements and analysis
- Correlation with Annemarie's presence

**Resolution**: Phenomena only occurred during office hours when Annemarie was present. Activity ceased when she left employment. Annemarie was reportedly unhappy with job and experiencing stress.

**Credibility**: High — Extensive scientific investigation; documented electrical anomalies; multiple professional witnesses; no fraud discovered; clear correlation with agent's presence.

**Significance**: Considered one of best-documented cases due to scientific rigor and professional investigation.

### Bell Witch (1817-1821)

> **Location**: Red River community, Robertson County, Tennessee, USA
> **Duration**: 4 years
> **Focal Person**: Betsy Bell (age 12-16 during activity)

**The Case**: One of America's most famous poltergeist cases. Entity calling itself "Kate" manifested in home of John Bell:

- Disembodied voices (multiple, speaking simultaneously)
- Physical attacks (slapping, hair pulling, needle sticks)
- Object movement
- Bedclothes pulled off
- Choking sensations
- Prophecies and knowledge of distant events
- Singing and laughing
- Death of John Bell (allegedly poisoned by entity)

**Notable Aspects**:
- Entity conversed with witnesses, demonstrated intelligence
- Claimed to be spirit of Kate Batts, a neighbor
- Predicted future events
- Allegedly visited by Andrew Jackson (disputed)
- Entity promised to return in 107 years (1935)

**Credibility**: Low-Medium — Most accounts written decades after events; impossible to verify many claims; folkloric embellishment likely; some contemporary documentation exists; historical distance makes verification impossible.

**Cultural Impact**: Most famous American poltergeist case; inspired books, films, and tourist industry in Tennessee; became part of American folklore.

---

### Amityville Horror (1975-1976) ⚠️

> **Location**: 112 Ocean Avenue, Amityville, Long Island, New York, USA
> **Duration**: 28 days (claimed)
> **Status**: WIDELY DISCREDITED AS HOAX

**Why Included**: Despite being proven fraudulent, included as cautionary example and due to massive cultural impact.

**The Claims**: Lutz family claimed terrifying paranormal activity in home (site of DeFeo family murders):
- Green slime oozing from walls
- Swarms of flies in winter
- Demonic apparitions
- Moving furniture
- Personality changes
- Levitation
- Hoofprints in snow

**The Fraud**:
- Lawyer William Weber admitted helping Lutzes concoct story "over wine"
- Neighbors reported no disturbances
- Family priest disputed account
- Polygraph tests failed or avoided
- Story changed in multiple retellings
- Financial motive (book and film deals)
- Subsequent owners reported nothing unusual
- Investigation revealed extensive fabrication

**Credibility**: Very Low — Admitted hoax; lawyer confession; inconsistent accounts; no physical evidence; financial motive.

**Cultural Impact**: Massive despite being fraud: bestselling book (1977), blockbuster film (1979), numerous sequels; became archetypal haunted house story.

**Lesson**: Demonstrates importance of critical investigation; financial incentives for fabrication; media sensationalism; human capacity for deception.

### Borley Rectory (1863-1944)

> **Location**: Borley, Essex, England
> **Duration**: Reported over 81 years
> **Primary Investigator**: Harry Price

**The Case**: Dubbed "most haunted house in England," the rectory experienced decades of reported phenomena:
- Phantom nun sightings
- Unexplained footsteps and raps
- Bell ringing
- Wall writing
- Object movement
- Phantom coach
- Spontaneous fires

**The Problem**: Subsequent SPR investigation revealed Harry Price likely exaggerated, misrepresented, and possibly fabricated evidence. Witnesses recanted; Price's methods questioned; much evidence disputed.

**Credibility**: Low — Price's claims largely discredited; fraud suspected; some core incidents may be genuine but unverifiable.

**Significance**: Cautionary tale about investigator bias and fraud.

## Theories & Explanations

### Paranormal Theories

#### 1. Disembodied Spirit Theory
**Description**: Activity caused by spirit of deceased person, often one who died traumatically or has unfinished business.

**Proponents**: Traditional paranormal investigators, spiritualists

**Evidence Cited**:
- Cases where entity identifies itself
- Communicative phenomena (rapping answers)
- Location-specific activity

**Criticisms**:
- Doesn't explain agent-focused pattern
- No scientific evidence for survival after death
- Many cases have no identified deceased person

#### 2. Demonic/Inhuman Entity Theory
**Description**: Activity caused by non-human malevolent entities (demons) targeting vulnerable individuals.

**Proponents**: Demonologists, religious investigators (e.g., Ed and Lorraine Warren)

**Evidence Cited**:
- Malevolent/violent phenomena
- Religious significance
- Entity self-identifies as demonic
- Cessation after religious intervention

**Criticisms**:
- Based on religious belief rather than evidence
- Same phenomena occur across cultures with different belief systems
- Psychological explanations more parsimonious

---

### Parapsychological Theory

#### RSPK - Recurrent Spontaneous Psychokinesis ⭐

**Description**: The leading parapsychological theory, developed by William G. Roll. Poltergeist phenomena are unconscious psychokinetic (PK) effects produced by a living agent (focal person), typically an adolescent under psychological or emotional stress. The agent unknowingly causes physical effects through mental processes during emotional turmoil.

**Developer**: William G. Roll (Psychical Research Foundation)

**Key Principles**:
- Focal person unconsciously produces phenomena through psychokinesis
- Triggered by psychological stress, repressed emotions
- Agent unaware they are causing effects
- Manifestation of internal psychological conflict
- Ceases when stress resolves or agent matures

**Supporting Evidence**:
- 90% of cases have identifiable agent
- Strong correlation with age 12-15 (puberty)
- Correlation with psychological stress, family conflict
- Activity follows agent from location to location
- Ceases when agent's stress resolves
- Phenomena occur in agent's presence or proximity
- Psychological testing shows higher stress in agents
- EEG anomalies in some agents (Persinger)

**Research Findings** (William Roll):
- Investigated 116 cases: found agent in 92%
- Peak age 12-15
- Higher incidence of family dysfunction
- Correlation with repressed emotions
- Temporal lobe activity patterns

**Strengths**:
- Explains agent-focused pattern
- Accounts for adolescent correlation
- Explains cessation when stress resolves
- Fits psychological profiles

**Criticisms**:
- Psychokinesis not scientifically established
- Mechanism unexplained
- Some phenomena occur outside agent's presence
- Doesn't explain intelligent communication
- No repeatable laboratory demonstration

---

### Psychological Theories

#### 1. Pubescent Stress Manifestation
**Description**: Phenomena are entirely psychological—combination of unconscious fraud, attention-seeking, and family dynamics involving stressed adolescents who create phenomena through trickery.

**Proponents**: Psychologists, family therapists, skeptical researchers

**Psychological Factors**:
- Puberty and hormonal changes
- Family conflict and dysfunction
- Divorce or parental separation
- Abuse or trauma history
- Inability to express emotions verbally
- Need for attention or control
- Repressed anger seeking outlet

**Evidence Cited**:
- High correlation with troubled adolescents
- Family dysfunction in most cases
- Many cases involve proven trickery
- Phenomena stop under continuous observation
- Attention-seeking behavior patterns

**Criticisms**:
- Doesn't explain all witnessed phenomena
- Some events occur with continuous observation
- Multiple independent witnesses in some cases

#### 2. Mass Hysteria/Suggestion
**Description**: Group suggestibility causes witnesses to misinterpret normal events or experience shared delusions.

**Evidence Cited**:
- Contagion of belief in affected households
- Phenomena increase with attention/investigation
- Accounts become more dramatic over time

---

### Skeptical Explanations

#### 1. Fraud and Hoax ⚠️
**Description**: Most or all cases are deliberate hoaxes for attention, fame, or financial gain.

**Proponents**: James Randi, Joe Nickell, skeptical investigators

**Evidence**:
- Numerous proven hoaxes (Fox Sisters, Amityville, etc.)
- Financial motives (book deals, media)
- Agents caught in trickery
- Phenomena cease under controlled observation
- Simple tricks can create impressive effects

**Known Techniques**:
- Throwing objects when unobserved
- Fishing line to move items
- Hidden accomplices
- Ventriloquism for voices
- Pre-staging events
- Exploiting investigation limitations

**Famous Exposures**:
- Fox Sisters admitted fraud (1888)
- Amityville proven fabrication
- Enfield children caught faking some phenomena
- Many cases collapse under scrutiny

**Criticisms**:
- Doesn't explain all cases satisfactorily
- Some phenomena witnessed under conditions preventing trickery
- Assumes all cases fraudulent without examination

#### 2. Misinterpretation of Normal Events
**Description**: Natural phenomena misinterpreted as paranormal.

**Natural Explanations**:
- House settling (creaking, movement)
- Plumbing (knocking sounds)
- Electrical surges
- Animals in walls (rodents, birds)
- Wind, temperature changes
- Underground streams, soil movement
- Infrasound (causes unease, hallucinations)

#### 3. Investigator Fraud/Bias
**Description**: Investigators themselves fabricate or exaggerate evidence.

**Examples**:
- Harry Price and Borley Rectory
- Financial incentives for investigators
- Confirmation bias
- Lack of proper controls

## Investigation Methods

### ⚠️ Important Warnings

**Safety**: Poltergeist investigation can be dangerous due to potential for physical harm (thrown objects, fires).

**Ethics**: Psychological well-being of focal person (often a child) must be prioritized. Many cases involve fraud—maintain critical perspective.

### Identification Criteria

Determining if case is genuine poltergeist vs. normal causes or fraud:

- ✓ Recurrent physical phenomena (object movement, sounds)
- ✓ Identifiable focal person (usually adolescent)
- ✓ Correlation between phenomena and focal person's presence
- ✓ Escalation pattern over time
- ✓ Psychological stress in focal person or family
- ✓ Rule out normal explanations (plumbing, animals, structural)
- ✓ Rule out fraud through observation and controls

### Investigation Approaches

| Method | Description | Equipment | Purpose |
|--------|-------------|-----------|---------|
| **Continuous Observation** | Maintain presence; observe directly | Notebooks, cameras, video | Document phenomena; detect trickery |
| **Environmental Monitoring** | Rule out normal causes | EMF meters, thermal cameras, engineers | Eliminate natural explanations |
| **Audio/Video Documentation** | Record phenomena | Video cameras (multiple angles), audio recorders | Provide evidence; detect fraud |
| **Controlled Experiments** | Test under controlled conditions | Sealed rooms, continuous monitoring | Determine genuine vs. hoaxed |
| **Psychological Assessment** | Evaluate focal person's mental state | Testing, interviews, family assessment | Understand stress factors; identify RSPK |
| **Witness Interviews** | Document accounts separately | Interview protocols, recording | Establish consistency and credibility |

### Red Flags for Fraud

- ⚠️ Phenomena only occur when focal person alone
- ⚠️ Elaborate phenomena impossible without preparation
- ⚠️ Agent caught in contradictions
- ⚠️ Financial motives present
- ⚠️ History of attention-seeking
- ⚠️ Phenomena conveniently avoid cameras
- ⚠️ Overly dramatic or Hollywood-like events
- ⚠️ Agent resists controlled testing
- ⚠️ Phenomena match popular media exactly

### Ethical Considerations

- Protect focal person (often minor) from exploitation
- Avoid causing additional psychological harm
- Maintain family privacy
- Don't assume fraud without evidence
- Don't validate delusions if mental illness present
- Involve mental health professionals when appropriate
- Avoid media sensationalism
- Obtain informed consent

### Historical Context

Poltergeist-like phenomena appear in folklore and historical records worldwide:

**Ancient References**:
- Roman accounts (Pliny the Younger)
- Chinese historical records
- Medieval European documentation

**Folklore Traditions**:
- German poltergeist folklore (origin of term)

## How to Report a Case

If you are experiencing or investigating poltergeist activity:

### 1. Documentation
- Record dates, times, and descriptions of all phenomena
- Take photographs and videos when possible
- Note correlation with specific individuals
- Document psychological state of potential focal person
- Maintain detailed logs

### 2. Rule Out Normal Causes
- Have qualified professionals check:
  - Electrical systems
  - Plumbing
  - Structural integrity
  - Pest infestation
- Document environmental factors

### 3. Seek Appropriate Help
- **If focal person is a minor or distressed**: Consult mental health professional first
- **If investigation desired**: Contact reputable research organization (SPR, Parapsychological Association)
- **Avoid**: Sensationalist "ghost hunters" or those seeking publicity

### 4. Submit Report
- **GitHub Issue**: [Submit Case Report](../../issues/new?template=case_report.yml)
- **Required Information**:
  - Detailed chronology
  - Witness information
  - Focal person details (if identifiable)
  - Evidence (photos, video, audio)
  - Professional assessments
